So it turns out, there's more than one way to write a number in Roman numerals. Are you surprised to learn that in ancient Rome, the use of Roman numerals was somewhat varied and inconsistent (can you smell my sarcasm?) In any case, today I learned that there was a version of Roman numerals that was used widely in inscriptions and official texts that did not make use of the subtractive notation we associate with Roman numerals today (IV for 4, IX for 9, etc.) I wrote a little program that takes in an Arabic number, given by a user, and returns the Roman numeral for that number, based on your choice of old Roman numerals, or modern Roman numerals that make use of subtractive notation.

To start, I made a hash of the base values that correspond to Roman numerals, and their associated notation. I also set up a method, convert_to_roman, that will take in an Arabic number

Next, I'll need to write a method that, given a number, goes through this hash and determines which notations to use, and how many. If the number passed in is 200, let's say, I know that my Roman numeral should be "CC." If I'm passing in 3,000, my output should be "MMM." The trick here is figuring out how many times one of the keys in my hash $letters goes in to the number I'm passing in.

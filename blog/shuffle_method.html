If you ever want to try to understand, or at least really appreciate what goes in to Ruby's beautiful built-in methods, try writing your own! Today I attempted to write a custom shuffle method in Ruby. While I certainly cannot guarantee this is how .shuffle() actually works, it gets the job done.

The first thing that comes to mind when I think of shuffling a collection of information, is "random." We're taking a set of data, whether it's numbers, names, etc., and rearraging the items in the set in RANDOM order. Utilizing Ruby's rand() method will be really important to us in this sense. To start, I create a method called "shuffle," which takes an array. Think about shuffling an array as taking all of its elements and putting them in random positions in a new array. Therefore, the random numbers that we're about to generate could be considered the NEW INDICES that the original elements of our array will get in a NEW ARRAY.

Yikes.

Since the subtractive notation really only affects instances of 9, 4, 90, 40, 900, and 400 (after 3000, modern Roman numerals require a horizontal line above them to indicate multiples of 1,000, which I won't be covering in this challenge), those are the numbers I'll need to target. My keys are instances where I would have 4 "I's" in a row for 4, a "VIIII" instad of a 9, and so on, with their proper replacements based on subtractive notation. Also, it's important to put the VIIII and LXXXX before IIII and XXXX in the hash, since when we iterate over those, it could see the "IIII" first, when what actually needs to be changed in our number is "VIIII."
